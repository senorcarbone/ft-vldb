\begin{abstract}
Stream processors often serve today as the main backbone for analytical but also highly critical services that encapsulate the core of persistent application logic in production. Thus, apart from low-latency, an emerging need is to provide strong consistency guarantees and fast adaptivity to cluster reconfigurations, software patches and failures that might potentially occur. Prior systems research has addressed several of these problems, however, the real challenge lies on how such guarantees can be materialized in a transparent, non-intrusive manner that relieves the user from unnecessary constraints. Such needs served as the main design principles of our state management work in Apache Flink\textsuperscript{\textregistered}, an open source, scalable stream processor. 

At Flink's core, an asynchronous in-band, snapshotting mechanism guarantees the creation of minimal, consistent distributed savepoints uninterruptedly. Savepoints are used for system reconfiguration, fault tolerance and version management through standard rollback recovery. Dataflow state is explicit, partitionable and transparently committed to persistent storage. We present Flink's lightweight mechanisms for state consistency, high availability and output commit while demonstrating how these mechanisms behave in practice with insights and associate trade-offs taken from real large-scale deployment experiences. Our evaluation analysis demonstrates low performance trade-offs of our techniques and the benefits of exploiting asynchrony in continuous, yet sustainable system deployments.
\end{abstract}
